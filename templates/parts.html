Here is a guide to all the parts your PC needs

CPU

The central-processing unit, or CPU, is the brain of the PC. The CPU processes and executes instructions for the hardware and software running on the PC.There are plenty of processors to choose from, but before you start building, you'll have to choose if you want an AMD or an Intel processor. This choice defines which motherboard you will have to get.

You can learn more about AMD and Intel processors through these websites:

  • AMD
  • Intel

Motherboard

The motherboard of a PC helps connect all the parts together so that they can communicate with each other. The motherboard also helps distribute power from the power supply to each component. The motherboard also helps define types of storage devices and memory. It also aids in the installation of the operating system on your machine. Some motherboards have extra features that is available to those that wish to push the performance of their machine to the max and much more. There are plenty of motherboards to choose from, but whichever motherboard you choose you'll want to make sure it is compatible with your CPU.

GPU

The graphics-processing unit or GPU in your PC helps handle graphics, effects, videos, and other processes of that nature. Some CPU's that you can purchase have built in graphics, so a GPU is not always necessary in a machine. However, if you wish to have a PC that is higher performance, and you'll be using it to do video editing, gaming, or other higher intensity tasks, you'll want to look into getting a graphics card for your machine.

You can learn more about graphics cards using these links:

  • AMD Radeon
  • NVIDIA GeForce

Memory

Memory, or random access memory(RAM) stores data while the computer runs. When a computer needs to access data quickly, it uses memory to do so. Processes that use memory include loading applications, browsing the internet, playing video games, editing word documents, and so on. The amount of memory you need varies depending on the kind of system you are building. For general office use you may only need 8GB of RAM. If you plan on gaming, you may need 16GB or more.

Storage

There are two different types of storage devices you can add to your system. One of these devices is called a hard disk drive or HDD, the other is called a solid state drive or SSD. HDDs and SSDs come with their own pros and cons.

Power Supply

The power supply in a PC provides power to all the components in the system. The most important part about deciding on a power supply is making sure you get the right wattage. How much power you need for your system depends on the components that you have inside. Newegg has a great tool that takes into account the parts you have in your machine, and calculates the necessary wattage based on that.
You can find this tool here: Newegg Power Supply Calculator

Operating System

In order to run your PC, you'll need an operating system to navigate through your system. An operating system, or OS, plays an important role in managing the computer's memory and processes, as well as all of its software and hardware. There are two different operating systems to choose from. If you want to go with the free option, Linux is an excellent choice. There are an exceptional amount of distributions to choose from when it comes to Linux however, and it isn't the most compatible OS for gaming. The other option for an OS is Windows. Windows is the most common OS for desktops as it has a lot of compatibility with a great deal of programs as well as gaming. Windows, however, you do have to pay for so do keep that in mind when making your decision.

Cooling

Cooling is an important part of a PC build as well. The components inside a PC generate a lot of heat, especially the CPU and GPU, so it is important to have a solution to keeping the inside of your system cool to prevent overheating. There are two ways you can choose to keep your system cool which includes either water cooling or making use of plenty of fans. With fans, you have to take into account what kind you'll need for your CPU and where you can place extra fans within your PC case. Some CPUs come with a fan that you can install, however with higher performance CPUs you will most likely need to buy a higher performance fan as a result. GPUs normally already have fans attached to them, so it's not usually necessary to buy fans for that component. To increase airflow in your system, you'll also want to attach fans to the front back and sometimes the top of your case. Water cooling a PC tends to be a more complicated and more expensive process but the payoff is fantastic. With water cooling, your PC runs a lot cooler than it would with fans, and since you don't have many fans running in your system, your PC runs a lot quieter as a result. Water cooling requires a lot of different parts to install, however. These parts include a radiator with fans to move heat from liquid into air, a water block to transfer heat into liquid, a pump to move the liquid, and a reservoir for automatically filtering air from the liquid and storing excess coolant.

PC Case

A PC case helps hold all the components of your system. The size of the case you need depends on the parts that you get. If you're building a high performance gaming rig, you probably need a bigger case to handle the size of the graphics card and cooling. Air flow is also another factor to consider in choosing a case. A lot of cases come with fans which can help cut down on the cost of having to buy more fans to install. If you are building a high performance rig, it can be helpful to choose a case that has a lot of places for you to install fans in your system. Computer case sizes are typically based on the type of motherboard you get, so when choosing your case, make sure it's compatible with your motherboard size.
